Solar vs Battery: Which Pays Back Faster?

Short answer: solar almost always pays back faster — typically 4–7 years, versus 7–12+ years for a battery. They're not really competing choices; solar usually comes first, and a battery becomes worthwhile afterwards, once there's enough spare solar export to actually store.

It's tempting to think of these as two options to pick between. In practice they solve different problems, and the order you install them in changes the numbers significantly.

Why solar is almost always faster

Solar reduces the cost of all your daytime electricity use — not just a portion of it. A battery, on the other hand, only captures the price difference between what you'd otherwise export at a low feed-in tariff and what you'd otherwise pay at the evening peak rate. That's a real saving, but on a narrower slice of your energy — which is why it typically takes longer to pay back.

Battery saving is based on shifting spare solar export (capped by typical annual cycling) from a ~5c/kWh feed-in tariff to a ~48c/kWh evening peak rate[1] — the same logic used throughout this site. Costs based on ~$900/kW solar and ~$950/kWh battery, both after current rebates[2][3].

When this changes

  • You're on a special off-peak or "free period" tariff — some retailers let you charge a battery cheaply from the grid, not just from solar export. This can make a battery worthwhile even with little or no solar, though it needs a genuinely low off-peak rate to work
  • You already have solar — the comparison above assumes installing solar first; if you already have it, the battery's payback depends entirely on how much you're currently exporting rather than using
  • You factor in financing costs — if you're borrowing to fund either upgrade, or giving up investment returns on the cash, both paybacks stretch out, and the gap between them can widen further since the battery's smaller annual saving is more sensitive to this
