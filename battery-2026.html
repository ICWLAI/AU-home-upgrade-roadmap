Should I Install a Home Battery in 2026?

Short answer: better than a couple of years ago thanks to the new federal rebate, but still usually the slowest-paying-back upgrade on this site — typically 7–12+ years. It makes most sense if you already have solar generating spare export, or you're on a tariff that lets you charge cheaply from the grid.

What changed in 2026

The federal Cheaper Home Batteries Program, in effect since 1 May 2026, brings typical installed costs down to roughly $800–$1,000 per kWh — around a 30% discount versus pre-rebate pricing[1]. That's a genuine improvement, but batteries remain a bigger upfront cost than solar or heat pump hot water for a smaller annual saving, so the payback stays comparatively long.

When it genuinely makes sense — and when it doesn't

✓ Good fit
  • You already have solar generating meaningful daily export
  • You're on a time-of-use tariff with a real peak/off-peak price gap
  • You have access to a genuinely cheap or free off-peak charging window
  • You value blackout backup enough to accept a longer payback
✕ Poor fit right now
  • You have little or no solar, and no off-peak/free-period tariff
  • Your household uses most of its power during the day already
  • You haven't yet installed solar or a heat pump — both usually pay back faster
  • You're financing the purchase at a meaningful interest rate
