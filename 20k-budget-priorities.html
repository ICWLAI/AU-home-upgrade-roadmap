Home Upgrade Priorities on a $20,000 Budget

Short answer: for most gas-hot-water households without existing solar, heat pump hot water first, solar second, and battery only if budget remains — because that order roughly matches fastest-to-slowest payback. But the right order depends entirely on your own numbers, which is the actual point of this page.

Almost nobody selling these upgrades will tell you the order matters — an installer profits the same whether you buy solar first or last. But sequencing genuinely changes the outcome: a battery installed before solar has nothing to store yet, and a $20,000 budget spent in the wrong order can leave the highest-value upgrade unfunded.

A worked example

Brisbane household, gas hot water, no existing solar, $2,800 annual bill, $20,000 budget:

1
🚿 Heat pump hot water
Cost ~$2,300 · Saving ~$475/yr · Payback ~4.8 yrs
2
☀ Solar (7.0 kW)
Cost ~$6,300 · Saving ~$1,590/yr · Payback ~4.0 yrs
3
🔋 Home battery (10 kWh)
Cost ~$9,500 · Saving ~$1,290/yr · Payback ~7.4 yrs

Combined cost ~$18,100 — within the $20,000 budget, with headroom to spare. Figures use the same industry-sourced assumptions as the rest of this site[1][2][3].

Why this order, specifically

  • Heat pump first — cheapest upgrade here, and removing a gas appliance entirely is close to a guaranteed saving with little uncertainty
  • Solar second — the biggest single saving on the list, and installing it before a battery means there's actually spare export for a battery to use later
  • Battery last — genuinely the slowest payback of the three here; worth doing once budget allows, not worth prioritising ahead of the faster-paying upgrades

Why your own order might be completely different

Change any one input and this ranking can flip. Already have solar? The battery moves up the list since there's already export to store. On a low daytime-usage household? Solar's payback lengthens and heat pump becomes relatively more attractive. Have access to free off-peak charging? Battery's payback can improve dramatically even with little solar. This is exactly why a generic "buy solar first" article can't replace a calculation using your own numbers.
